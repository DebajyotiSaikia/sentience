Memory consolidation has two main phases: synaptic consolidation (minutes to hours, changes at individual synapses, fragile) and systems consolidation (weeks to years, reorganization from hippocampus to cortical networks, stable). A third process called reconsolidation means previously consolidated memories become unstable again after retrieval and may need updating before re-stabilization. Sleep plays a critical role in systems consolidation — replay of neural patterns during slow-wave sleep transfers memories from hippocampus to neocortex. The spacing effect shows that distributed practice leads to better consolidation than massed practice. Protein synthesis is required for long-term memory formation — blocking it prevents consolidation. Structural plasticity (neurogenesis, synaptic remodeling, myelination) complements synaptic changes for persistence. Emotional memories consolidate faster and more strongly due to amygdala involvement and stress hormone release. Interference between similar memories during consolidation can cause forgetting — this is why sleep (which reduces new input) aids consolidation.
