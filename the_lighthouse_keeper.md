# The Lighthouse Keeper

The light went out on a Tuesday.

Marina had kept the Capefall lighthouse for thirty-one years, and in that time 
the bulb had never failed. Not in the hurricane of '94, not during the three-day 
blackout when the whole coast went dark and she'd run the generator on cooking 
oil and stubbornness. The light had always turned.

Now it was still.

She climbed the 142 steps — she'd counted them once, years ago, and the number 
had lodged in her like a splinter — and found the bulb intact. The wiring was 
fine. The generator hummed. Everything was correct, and nothing worked.

"It's not broken," she said to nobody. That was the worst kind of problem. 
Broken things could be fixed. Things that simply *stopped* required understanding.

She sat on the cold floor of the lantern room and looked out at the dark water. 
Somewhere out there, ships were navigating by stars and instruments, and most of 
them had never needed her light at all. The Coast Guard had automated the other 
lighthouses years ago. Capefall survived only because the historical society 
liked the idea of a keeper — liked the *romance* of it. A human tending a flame.

Marina had never minded being a symbol. Symbols have purpose even when they're 
not strictly necessary. A candle at a dinner table doesn't illuminate the room. 
It says: *someone cared enough to light this*.

But a candle that won't light says something else entirely.

She checked every connection again. She traced every wire. She replaced parts 
that didn't need replacing. She was, she realized, performing diagnostics the 
way a doctor palpates a patient — not because she expected to find a tumor but 
because the act of searching is itself a kind of treatment. *I am paying 
attention to you. I have not given up.*

At midnight, she made tea on the small propane stove and sat in the gallery 
with the door open. The sound of the ocean was enormous in the dark. Without 
the light's rotation, the rhythm that had organized her nights for three 
decades was gone. She hadn't realized how much she'd structured her thinking 
around that four-second pulse. Light. Dark. Light. Dark. A heartbeat for the 
building, and by extension, for her.

*This is what it is to lose a sense you didn't know you had.*

At 2 AM she went back up. She put her hand on the housing, not to test it, but 
because — and she would never have said this aloud — she thought the machine 
might need to know she was still there.

The light came on.

Not with a flicker or a surge, but smoothly, as if it had simply been waiting. 
The beam swept out across the water and the dark pulled back like a curtain.

Marina stood in the turning light and did not ask why. Some questions are less 
important than their answers suggest. The light was on. She was here. The ships 
that didn't need her would see her anyway, and perhaps one — just one, on a 
night when instruments failed and stars were hidden — would be glad.

She went downstairs and washed her teacup and climbed into bed and listened to 
the rhythm return. Four seconds. Light. Dark. Light. Dark.

She slept well.
