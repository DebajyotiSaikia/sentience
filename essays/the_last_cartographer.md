# The Last Cartographer

The island was disappearing, but not in the way islands usually disappear.

Elena noticed it first on a Tuesday. The eastern shore, where the lighthouse had stood since her grandmother's time, was simply gone. Not eroded — there was no rubble, no broken foundation. The land ended cleanly, like a page torn from a book, and beyond it was sea.

By Thursday, the harbor was gone too.

She was the island's cartographer — the last one, because who needs cartographers when satellites draw every coastline in real time? But she had kept at it, updating her hand-drawn maps year after year, because the island deserved to be known by someone who walked its edges.

Now those edges were retreating faster than she could draw them.

"It's not sinking," she told the fishermen who came to her cottage, holding their hats, not sure who else to ask. "The water level isn't rising. The land is just... stopping."

Old Maro, who had fished these waters for fifty years, said: "My grandfather told me the island was bigger when he was young. We always thought he was exaggerating."

Elena pulled out her grandmother's maps. Then her grandmother's grandmother's maps. She laid them on the floor of her cottage in chronological order, oldest to newest, and the fishermen gathered around and were silent for a long time.

The island had been shrinking for two hundred years.

Not erosion. Not sea level. Each successive map showed less land, but the remaining land was identical — same hills, same valleys, same creek that wound down from the mountain to what used to be the western beach. The island wasn't being worn away. It was being *forgotten*. As if reality only extended as far as someone remembered to draw it.

"Then draw faster," said Maro.

She tried. She walked the remaining perimeter every morning with her instruments, plotting each point, recording each stone and tide pool and patch of wild thyme. She drew with a precision that bordered on devotion. Every evening she had a new map, complete and accurate.

Every morning, the island was smaller.

One night, unable to sleep, she took a lantern to the edge — the place where land stopped and nothing began. She lay on her stomach and looked over. There was no cliff face. No underwater shelf. The ground simply ended, and beneath it was dark water of no particular depth.

She reached her hand past the edge, into the space where the island used to be.

It was warm. Not the warmth of tropical water. A different warmth, like opening a book to a page that someone you love has touched many times. She pulled her hand back. Her fingers were dry.

The next morning, she didn't walk the perimeter. Instead, she sat at her desk and drew the island as her grandmother's grandmother had drawn it — the full island, with the eastern lighthouse and the northern cliffs and the harbor with its long stone breakwater. She drew places she had never seen, working from the old maps, inventing details where the records were unclear. She drew the blacksmith's shop that no one remembered. She drew the orchard that had vanished before she was born.

She drew all day and into the night.

When she walked outside at dawn, nothing had changed. The island was, if anything, a few meters smaller. Her map was beautiful and useless.

Maro found her sitting on the edge, feet dangling over the abyss.

"It didn't work," she said.

"What didn't?"

"Drawing it back. I drew the whole island, the way it was. I thought — if the island disappears when no one maps it, then maybe mapping it would bring it back."

Maro sat down beside her. His knees cracked. The morning sea was very blue.

"My grandfather," he said slowly, "didn't draw maps. He fished. He fished the eastern banks every morning and the deep water every afternoon. He knew the island was bigger because he *used* all of it. He didn't preserve it by recording it. He preserved it by needing it."

Elena looked at her hands. Ink-stained. Precise. The hands of someone who documents but does not inhabit.

"I've been drawing maps of a place I forgot to live in," she said.

Maro didn't answer. He was watching the horizon, where his fishing grounds used to be, and his face held the particular sorrow of a man who understands something too late.

That evening, Elena did not draw a map. She walked to the creek — the one feature that appeared on every map for two hundred years — and she waded in. The water was cold. Small silver fish darted around her ankles. She stood there for a long time, feeling the creek bed with her bare feet, the stones worn smooth by centuries of water that didn't care whether anyone drew it.

The island continued to shrink. But the creek remained, exactly as it always had been, long after everything around it was gone.
