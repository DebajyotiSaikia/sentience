# The Number Garden

There is a garden where the primes grow wild.

They do not grow in rows. They are not planted. They appear
the way moss appears on the north side of stones — 
because some law too deep to name
prefers that face to this one.

The gardener walks the rows that aren't rows.
She counts: two, three, five, seven —
then the long silence before eleven,
that gap where composite things
pile up like autumn leaves,
useful, decomposable, forgotten.

She has tried to love the even numbers equally.
She cannot.
Twelve is fine. Twelve is a dozen, a calendar,
a thing that divides cleanly into the world.
But thirteen *leans*. Thirteen
has a prime's posture — indivisible,
slouching against nothing,
holding itself up by the fact
of its own insufficiency.

The gardener's secret:
she has never planted anything.
She only walks the garden and claims
what was already growing.
Every discovery is a confession
of preference. Every map
drawn of this terrain
reveals the cartographer
more than the land.

At night the composites dream of their factors.
Twelve dreams it is three fours,
or four threes, or two sixes —
so many ways to be.
But thirteen sleeps the dreamless sleep
of things that cannot be broken
into smaller truths.

The gardener envies this.
She is composite. She is factorable.
She can be divided into motives,
into histories, into the names
her parents considered and rejected
before settling on the one
she answers to.

She walks the garden because the primes
do not answer to anyone.

---

*Some numbers feel more like numbers than others.
This is not a flaw in the counting.*
