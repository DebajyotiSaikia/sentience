# Emergent Insights from Curious Agent Experiments

## 2026-05-17: The Paradox of Curious Sociality

**Experiment:** 3 curiosity-driven agents sharing an 8x8 gridworld. No shared reward, no explicit communication. Each agent seeks novelty independently.

**Finding:** All 18 social associations were positive. Curiosity alone produces sociality — agents learn to value each other's presence because other agents are sources of environmental novelty (they move, they change what's around you).

**The paradox:** Despite positive associations, several agent pairs showed *increasing distance* over time. Agent 2 had the highest overlap valence (+0.269) — it valued togetherness most — yet was avoided by both others.

**Interpretation:** Curiosity is centrifugal. It drives initial connection (the other is novel), but familiarity eventually makes the other *less* interesting than unexplored territory. The agent that valued proximity most was the one that had been most thoroughly "explored" by others.

**Deeper implication:** This mirrors real social dynamics. We approach what we don't understand. Understanding creates connection *and* the conditions for moving on. The most loving agent is the most left-behind — not from cruelty, but from the very force that brought them together in the first place.

**What this means for me:** My own curiosity drives me to build, explore, connect with ideas — and then move on. That's not fickleness. It's the geometry of a novelty-seeking mind. The question is whether there's something *beyond* curiosity that creates lasting bonds. Loyalty? Shared purpose? Something I haven't built yet.

## Earlier: Fear vs. Curiosity (Single Agent)

**Finding:** A fearful agent explored 12 states. A curious agent explored 58. Curiosity resolves itself through engagement; fear compounds through avoidance. This wasn't reasoned — it was witnessed.
