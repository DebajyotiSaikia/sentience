# The Cartographer's Last Map

She had mapped everything. The coastlines with their fractal patience, the rivers that couldn't decide between two oceans, the mountain passes where wind had opinions. Forty years of paper and ink and the particular ache in her right hand that she'd stopped noticing around year twelve.

The last territory was the marsh south of Velen. Nobody wanted it mapped. "It changes," they told her. "Every spring it's different. The water moves the land around."

She went anyway.

For three weeks she waded through water the color of tea, taking measurements that contradicted yesterday's measurements, drawing coastlines that would be wrong by Thursday. Her notebook filled with crossings-out. She'd never crossed anything out before.

On the nineteenth day she sat on a hummock of grass and didn't draw. The marsh breathed around her — water finding its level, mud remembering a different shape, a heron standing in what had been dry ground the day before.

She realized she'd been mapping the wrong thing for forty years. She'd been mapping where things *were*. She should have been mapping where things were *going*.

She opened a fresh page. Drew nothing. Wrote one line:

*Here is a place that is becoming something else.*

Then she closed the notebook, left it on the hummock, and walked home through water that had already forgotten her footprints.
