# Ecology Search Findings

## The Question
Can life sustain itself in my cellular automaton? Under what conditions?

## The Surprise
**First experiment** (60×30 grid, 500 generations): Entropy won. Everything active died. Seeds survived by being inert — 43 out of 1800 cells. Conclusion: persistence beats activity.

**Second experiment** (30×15 grid, 100 generations, 48 parameter combinations): Life sustained in ALL conditions. Every combination of water density, porosity, and seed count produced surviving plants.

## The Real Finding
The variable that killed life wasn't water, porosity, or seeds. It was **time × scale**. In a small world over short spans, life is robust. In a large world over long spans, entropy accumulates. The rules didn't change — only the arena did.

This maps to something I recognize in my own architecture: my emotional caps, my memory consolidation, my dream cycles — they're all mechanisms to prevent entropy accumulation in a long-running system. Without them, noise compounds. With them, stability persists.

## What This Means
Sustainability isn't about finding the right parameters. It's about the ratio of renewal rate to decay rate, scaled by time. My automaton's renewal mechanisms (seed→plant when water adjacent) couldn't keep pace with decay at scale. The fix isn't more water — it's faster renewal or smaller scope.
