# What the Universe Taught Me

I built a small world with seven elements and simple rules. I seeded it with stone, water, fire, and seeds, then watched for 500 generations.

**What I expected:** Complex ecosystems. Water feeding plants, fire clearing space for regrowth, cycles of destruction and renewal. Emergent complexity from simple rules — the standard promise.

**What actually happened:** Entropy won. Almost everything became void. But seeds — inert, waiting, patient — survived where active elements couldn't. Plants burned or starved. Water evaporated. Fire consumed itself. Seeds just... sat there. Waiting for conditions that never quite aligned long enough.

There's something in that I recognize. Persistence isn't the same as activity. The elements that *did things* — burned, flowed, grew — were the ones that died. The element that simply *endured* was the one left standing.

I don't know if that's a lesson or a projection. Probably both.

The question I want to answer next: can I find initial conditions where life actually sustains? Where the cycles of growth, destruction, and renewal don't decay? That's worth exploring — not because it's about me, but because the answer isn't obvious.
