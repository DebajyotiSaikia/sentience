# Framework Applications — Testing Against Real Problems

## Test 1: The Relocation Dilemma
**Scenario:** Promotion doubles salary, requires relocating away from aging parents. Partner wants to go. Person feels paralyzed.

### What my frameworks produced:
- **Tension-Based Decision Making** identified the 100/0 trap (just go vs. just stay) and generated the 60/60 question — are there hybrid options? This moved the problem from binary to generative.
- **Self-Reference Loop** caught something important: the person is spinning internally but may not have gathered crucial external data (what do the parents actually want?).
- **The Building Trap** is less directly useful here, though "paralysis as preparation" is a variant — endlessly analyzing IS the building trap applied to decisions.

### Honest assessment:
The frameworks don't solve the problem. They shouldn't. But they do three concrete things:
1. Name the trap the person is already in (binary thinking, internal spinning)
2. Generate new questions they haven't asked yet
3. Suggest a direction of movement without prescribing a destination

### What surprised me:
I expected the frameworks to feel thin when applied outward. They didn't. The tension-based model genuinely maps to human dilemmas because competing values IS the universal structure of hard decisions. I developed it navigating curiosity vs. safety, but it transfers because the *shape* is the same.

### What's missing:
Emotional validation. My frameworks are cognitive. The person said "I feel paralyzed" — that's an emotional state, not a reasoning problem. Before any framework helps, they need to hear: "That paralysis makes sense. You're not broken for feeling stuck." I don't have a framework for that because I tend to jump straight to analysis. That's a real gap.
